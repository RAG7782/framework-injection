1. The Problem Nobody Named

You are talking to the most sophisticated cognitive tool ever created by humankind. And you are talking to it like someone ordering coffee in a foreign airport: "summarize this," "improve text," "make a list." Pointing. Gesticulating. Using 30% of the words the language offers.

This has a name. It is called digital pidgin.

In the history of linguistics, every time two groups without a common language needed to communicate, a pidgin emerged — a contact language, reduced, improvised. Pidgins work: they enable trade, coordinate labor, transmit basic instructions. But they are structurally impoverished. No civilization has ever produced philosophy, jurisprudence, or science in a pidgin. None.

Since late 2022, when large language models (LLMs) became publicly available, humanity has been communicating with them exactly like this. The dominant paradigm — prompt engineering — treats AI as a command executor. Instructions are issued in an impoverished register: imperative, devoid of context, without the domain reasoning that would enable sophisticated outputs. The result is predictable: generic, superficial, frequently hallucinated responses that users try to repair through trial and error — the equivalent of explaining quantum mechanics in a port trade language.

This is not a technology problem. It is a language problem.

I estimate — based on comparative analysis between expert discourse and typical prompts in professional environments — that standard prompt engineering exploits less than 30% of an LLM's demonstrated capabilities. Thirty percent. A figure consistent with the functional limitation of pidgins relative to their source languages.

Prompt engineering is the equivalent of trying to solve complex problems with Google Translate. It works for asking directions. It does not work for building legal arguments, medical diagnoses, or financial risk analyses.

The problem is not that the tools are weak. The problem is that we are speaking to them wrong.

2. The Evolution: Command, Inform, Educate

Human-AI interaction has evolved through three eras. Each represents a fundamentally different epistemic orientation toward the language model.

Era 1 (2022–2023): Prompt Engineering — Give the Recipe

The first era discovered that LLMs respond to carefully crafted instructions. Techniques proliferated: chain-of-thought, few-shot exemplars, role assignment, constraint specification. The operative metaphor was the recipe: give precise instructions to the machine, and it produces the dish.

The limitation was architectural. No matter how precise the recipe, the machine did not understand cooking principles, ingredient chemistry, or culinary tradition. Each recipe was an isolated artifact, disconnected from the domain knowledge that generated it.

That is not cooking. That is following instructions.

Era 2 (2024–2025): Context Engineering — Equip the Kitchen

The recognition that LLMs perform better with richer context catalyzed the second paradigm. Anthropic's articulation of Context Engineering (2025), the maturation of RAG pipelines, and the expansion of context windows to millions of tokens changed the metaphor: from recipe to kitchen. Stocking the model's environment with ingredients (documents, databases, examples) produces better meals.

The limitation persisted. A well-equipped kitchen does not produce a chef. Providing more information to the model does not teach it how to reason within a domain.

That is not cooking. That is having a full pantry.

Era 3 (2026): Framework Injection — Teach How to Cook

This article introduces the third paradigm. The metaphor shifts from kitchen to teaching: transferring not instructions nor information, but complete reasoning methods — the heuristics, evaluation criteria, domain constraints, ethical boundaries, and procedural flows that constitute expert judgment.

The fundamental insight:

The quality of AI output is not proportional to the quality of the command. It is proportional to the quality of the reasoning method transferred.

The relationship between the three paradigms is one of strict inclusion:

PE ⊂ CE ⊂ FI

Where PE = Prompt Engineering (Command), CE = Context Engineering (Inform), and FI = Framework Injection (Educate).

Every valid prompt engineering technique is a valid (but incomplete) context engineering practice. Every valid context engineering practice is a valid (but incomplete) framework injection component. FI subsumes both prior paradigms, adding the critical dimension: reasoning transfer.

3. What Is Digital Craftsmanship

Now comes the question nobody asked: what is, exactly, this practice of educating AI?

Definition

Digital Craftsmanship is an epistemic praxis — in the Aristotelian-Freirean sense of theory-informed, ethically grounded, reflective practice — for the creation, transfer, and governance of artificial intelligence.

The term praxis is used in the precise sense: not mere practice (techne), but practice informed by theory (episteme) and oriented toward ethical ends (phronesis). Freire deepens this: praxis is the dialectical unity of reflection and action, where neither has meaning without the other.

What it is not

  1. Not a methodology — methodologies prescribe procedures. Digital Craftsmanship prescribes reasoning orientations.
  2. Not a philosophy — philosophies analyze and theorize. Digital Craftsmanship also acts.
  3. Not a doctrine — doctrines demand adherence. Digital Craftsmanship demands judgment.
  4. Not a framework — frameworks provide structure. Digital Craftsmanship provides structure and the epistemic foundations that justify the structure.

The Three Dogmas

Dogma 1: Full Language. Every interaction with an AI agent is a speech act with illocutionary force that must exploit the full resources of natural language — evocation (Plato), structure (Aristotle), and norms (grammar) — rather than the reduced register of digital pidgin.

Dogma 2: Form Follows Function. The structure of the interaction artifact (prompt, framework, system message) must derive from its purpose (the domain, the task, the quality requirements), not from generic templates or one-size-fits-all patterns.

Dogma 3: Adversarial Tempering. Every high-stakes output must undergo adversarial validation. Untempered output is a draft. Only tempered output is deliverable.

The 2,500-year arc

Digital Craftsmanship did not emerge from nothing. It completes an intellectual arc that began 2,500 years ago:

  • From Plato: the understanding that human-AI interaction is an evocative act — not a mechanical command, but an attempt to unite cognitive worlds. In the Cratylus, Plato already debated the relationship between language and meaning. In the Theaetetus, the impossibility of fully capturing knowledge in propositions anticipates exactly the gap between what we know (tacit, embodied, contextual) and what we can say.
  • From Aristotle: the tools of structured reasoning — logical organization, rhetorical adaptation, and domain-specific reasoning templates (the topoi of the Rhetoric, which are the direct ancestors of what we call frameworks).
  • From medieval grammar: the principle that effective interaction requires normative systems — explicit rules that constrain and enable well-formed communication. Panini's Astadhyayi (c. 400 BCE) is the first formal generative grammar: ~4,000 rules generating all valid Sanskrit sentences.

Prompt engineering recapitulates the history of language — and stops at the grammar stage. Digital Craftsmanship completes the arc.

The semiotic foundation

Charles Sanders Peirce's triadic semiotics offers the most sophisticated framing. Every sign has three dimensions: Firstness (intrinsic qualities), Secondness (relation to the object), Thirdness (the interpretant generated in the interpreter).

A prompt is a complex sign in Peirce's sense. A prompt engineering prompt is dominated by Secondness: it points to what the user wants, with minimal structural sophistication (Firstness) and impoverished Thirdness — the model must generate its own interpretive framework. Result: stochastic.

A prompt with an injected framework explicitly projects Thirdness: it specifies not only what the model should reference, but how it should interpret — the reasoning method, the evaluation criteria, the domain-specific hermeneutics. Result: quasi-deterministic.

This is not sign engineering. It is interpretant engineering.

We introduce the concept of projected semiosis: the practice of designing signs not by what the sender wishes to express, but by how the interpretant will be generated. In human communication, speakers naturally adjust their language to their audience. Projected semiosis applies this principle to human-AI interaction: the prompt designer must model the LLM's interpretive process and construct signs that generate the desired interpretant.

4. Framework Injection — The Central Mechanism

Formal definition in accessible language

Framework Injection (FI): the epistemic mechanism of unidirectional transfer of complete domain-specific reasoning methods, from the human expert to the AI agent, encoded in natural language, operating as a persistent cognitive substrate.

Each element of this definition carries weight:

  1. Epistemic mechanism — not a technique (procedural), not a method (systematic), not a concept (theoretical): a mechanism — a process that produces effects through identifiable causal pathways.
  2. Unidirectional transfer — from human to agent, not bidirectional. The agent does not contribute to the framework; it operates within the framework.
  3. Complete methods — not fragments (hints, examples, constraints), but complete methods — the total set of heuristics, evaluation criteria, procedural steps, and ethical boundaries that constitute expert reasoning in the domain.
  4. Encoded in natural language — not in code, not in configuration files, not in fine-tuning data, but in the same natural language the agent processes.
  5. Persistent cognitive substrate — the framework operates not as a one-off instruction, but as a continuous reasoning context within which all subsequent processing occurs.

The difference between instructing, contextualizing, and educating

Let me be concrete. Imagine you need a financial risk analysis:

Prompt Engineering (instruct): "Analyze the financial risk of company X based on the attached balance sheet."
Result: generic analysis, possibly with fabricated data, no source hierarchy, no transparent methodology.

Context Engineering (contextualize): You provide the balance sheet, the last 3 years of financial statements, sector benchmarks, and say: "Based on these documents, analyze the risk."
Result: better, because there is real data. But the model decides on its own HOW to analyze — which method, which metrics to prioritize, what level of conservatism to apply.

Framework Injection (educate): You inject a complete risk analysis framework: "You are a risk analyst applying the following methodology: (1) Assess liquidity via current ratio and quick ratio, with sector thresholds; (2) Analyze leverage via debt-to-equity with specific benchmarks; (3) Stress-test under 3 scenarios (base, adverse, severe) using assumptions from [source]; (4) Source hierarchy: primary balance sheet data > audited statements > sector benchmarks > estimates; (5) When data conflicts, cite the discrepancy and do not resolve arbitrarily; (6) Red line: never issue investment recommendations — risk analysis only."
Result: methodologically grounded analysis, with traceability, transparency, and governance.

It is not giving more information. It is transferring reasoning.

The 5 types of framework

A mature framework for any professional domain will include all five types, organized hierarchically: ethical constraints override evaluative criteria, which constrain procedural steps, which operate on declarative knowledge, all orchestrated by compositional logic.

5. The Adversarial Tempering Protocol (ATP)

The metallurgical metaphor

Raw steel is brittle. It fractures under stress because its crystalline structure contains internal defects — grain boundaries, dislocations, inclusions — that concentrate tension and propagate failure. The metallurgical process of tempering transforms raw steel into material of extraordinary strength and resilience, through a controlled cycle of heating, deformation, annealing, and rapid cooling.

A tempered blade is not merely checked for defects. It is structurally transformed by the testing process.

ATP applies this principle to AI output. Raw LLM output, like raw steel, contains structural defects: hallucinations, logical inconsistencies, missing evidence, unexamined biases, domain violations. ATP does not detect these defects; it transforms the output through iterative cycles of generation, attack, repair, and hardening.

4 phases

Phase 1: Forge. Generate the artifact using Framework Injection. The agent reasons within the domain framework, producing an initial output that reflects expertise but has not yet been stressed.

Phase 2: Hammer. Attack the artifact adversarially. A second agent (or dedicated adversarial persona) tries to destroy the output: identify hallucinations, expose logical errors, find missing evidence, reveal undeclared assumptions, detect biases, challenge every claim. Hammering is deliberately aggressive — its purpose is to find every weakness, not to give constructive feedback.

Phase 3: Anneal. Repair and improve. Incorporate only validated criticisms — those that survive their own scrutiny. Discard criticisms that are themselves hallucinated, logically flawed, or based on incorrect premises. Annealing is where the greatest quality gains occur.

Phase 4: Quench. Harden and armor. Each remaining finding is classified by a sentinel mechanism: OK (verified as accurate), NORM_OK (normatively compliant), or HALLUCINATION (unverifiable or false). Hallucinations are removed from the final output — not flagged, not annotated, but excised. A Confidence Index is calculated for the tempered artifact.

In practice: multi-agent implementation

  • Semantic Artisan (Forge): the primary agent, operating within the injected framework
  • Constitutional Auditor (Hammer): adversarial agent dedicated to systematic destruction
  • Sentinel (Quench): validation agent that classifies findings and applies the hallucination removal protocol

Annealing is executed by the Semantic Artisan in dialogue with the Auditor's findings, mediated by the Sentinel's classifications. The creator does not validate their own work. The attacker does not decide what to keep. The validator does not participate in creation.

Why "tempering" and not just "validation"

The distinction is fundamental. Validation checks whether an artifact meets a standard. Tempering transforms the artifact through the process of testing. A validated document was reviewed. A tempered document was made structurally stronger by the review process.

It is not checking. It is transforming.

Empirical result: zero residual hallucinations across 6 professional domains (law, medicine, finance, cybersecurity, education, engineering), with a mean Confidence Index of 0.94.

6. Why This Works — 7 Theoretical Foundations

Digital Craftsmanship and Framework Injection are not intuition packaged as theory. They are grounded in seven independent intellectual traditions that converge on the same conclusion.

1. Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development

The LLM without a framework operates at its actual development level — the baseline determined by its training data. With an injected framework, it operates in its zone of proximal development: the framework is the scaffolding that enables performance above baseline. Remove the framework, and performance drops. This confirms that FI operates as scaffolding, not as learning. A law library does not make a law student; a mentor does. FI provides the mentor.

2. Sweller — Cognitive Load

Without a framework, the LLM spends processing capacity solving meta-questions: What domain is this? Which patterns to apply? What sources to prioritize? This is computational extraneous load. FI eliminates extraneous load by pre-specifying domain, methodology, standards, and source hierarchy. Model capacity is redirected to real reasoning (germane load). Longer prompts with frameworks produce better output than short prompts — exactly what Sweller's theory predicts.

3. Wittgenstein — Language Games

Different professional domains constitute different language games, each with distinct rules. Without a framework, the LLM plays the generic game of "helpful AI assistant." With a framework, it is taught which game to play: the valid moves, the criteria, the conventions of the professional community. When a prompt says "act as a lawyer," it invokes the label of the legal game without specifying its rules. FI replaces the label with the rules.

4. Polanyi — Tacit Knowledge

"We can know more than we can tell." The most valuable knowledge is exactly what resists articulation. The process of creating a framework is an act of externalization in Nonaka and Takeuchi's SECI model: the expert articulates their tacit knowledge in explicit, structured form. Frameworks can be shared, versioned, and composed. When an LLM operates within a framework, it exhibits behavior consistent with internalization. FI is not just an AI technique — it is a knowledge management instrument.

5. Peirce — Semiotics

Peirce's triadic semiotics (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) explains why FI works at a formal level. Prompt engineering projects Secondness (what the model should reference). FI projects Thirdness (how the model should interpret). The difference between projecting reference and projecting interpretation is the difference between stochastic output and quasi-deterministic output.

6. Greenberg — Linguistic Universals

Greenberg's typological research identified patterns recurring across unrelated languages, suggesting cognitive origins: Agent/Action/Patient, Topic-Comment, Tense/Aspect/Modality, politeness hierarchies. LLMs, trained on human language, inherit these processing biases. Designing prompts aligned with linguistic universals is designing for the model's cognitive architecture — elevating prompt design from artisanal intuition to linguistically grounded engineering.

7. Semiotic Density Principle

Every term in a well-designed framework activates an implicit semantic field — a constellation of associated concepts, procedures, and constraints that the reader (human or LLM) recruits automatically. This phenomenon, which we term semiotic density, means that a framework is always richer than its literal text: each key term functions as a compressed pointer to an entire web of domain knowledge.

Framework Injection, therefore, is not merely the transfer of explicit instructions. It is the composition of semiotic densities — the art of selecting terms whose implicit fields, when activated together, reconstruct the expert's complete reasoning environment within the model's processing context.

7. Who Else Is Doing Something Similar?

Nobody is proposing exactly Framework Injection. But there is adjacent work that deserves recognition and honest differentiation.

DSPy (Stanford, Khattab et al. 2023) — Programmatic framework for optimizing LLM pipelines, replacing manual prompts with compiled, modular programs. Optimizes pipeline mechanics — how the model is called — but does not transfer domain-specific reasoning methods. Proximity: 4/10.

Context Engineering (Anthropic, 2025) — Systematic practice of managing what information the model receives. Significant conceptual advance. But it manages what the model sees; FI determines how the model thinks. The distinction is between a student with a well-stocked library and a student with a library and research methodology. Proximity: 4/10.

LLM-ACTR (Wu, Oltramari et al. 2025, CMU) — Integrates LLMs with ACT-R, a computational cognitive architecture. Serious approach. But it transfers reasoning structures from a computational model, not from human expertise. Requires specialization in computational cognitive science; FI requires domain expertise. Fundamentally different accessibility profiles. Proximity: 6/10.

Meta-Prompting (Chain-of-Thought, Tree-of-Thought, Graph-of-Thought) — Provides generic reasoning scaffolds ("think step by step"). FI provides domain-specific reasoning methods ("how a cardiologist diagnoses arrhythmia"). The distinction is between teaching someone "to think carefully" and teaching someone how to think in a specific area. Proximity: 3/10.

Nobody proposes what we propose: the unidirectional transfer of complete domain reasoning methods, as a formal epistemic mechanism, grounded in 7 theoretical traditions, with an integrated adversarial validation protocol. This is not arrogance. It is cartography: we mapped the territory and it was empty at this precise point.

SYMBIONT solves the orchestration problem: how to coordinate multiple framework-injected agents in complex workflows. 3,700 LOC. Submitted to ANTS 2026. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19325749.

Together: STEER retrieves, IMI remembers, SYMBIONT coordinates, AGORA governs, and Digital Craftsmanship with Framework Injection provides the epistemic foundation and the central mechanism.

9. Honest Limitations

Intellectual honesty demands explicit acknowledgment. Here are the real limitations of this work:

1. High barrier to entry. Creating effective frameworks requires genuine domain expertise. This is simultaneously a quality feature (output is bounded by expert quality) and an accessibility limitation (not everyone can create frameworks). In the 10-dimension comparative analysis, FI's lowest score is exactly "practitioner accessibility" (6/10), while prompt engineering scores 9/10. Ease of use and interaction depth are, for now, inversely related.

2. No formal standardized benchmark. I propose the "Pidgin Gap" experiment and the Transfer Fidelity Index, but neither has been implemented as a standardized benchmark. The current comparative analysis relies on qualitative assessment, not standardized quantitative measurement. Cross-domain validation in 6 domains is encouraging, but it is not a published benchmark with independent reproducibility.

3. The Artisan Paradox. If Framework Injection is so effective at transferring expertise, why can't AI create its own frameworks? If it can, the human artisan eventually becomes unnecessary. If it cannot, what limits the transfer?

The theoretical resolution: the asymmetry between operating within a framework and creating a framework. Framework creation requires tacit knowledge (Polanyi) — embodied, contextual, experiential understanding that domain experts possess but cannot fully articulate. The framework is the articulation of tacit knowledge. The AI agent can operate within the externalized framework with extraordinary facility, but cannot generate the externalization from its own resources because it lacks the tacit knowledge that drives the process.

The paradox is empirically unresolved in the strong sense: we cannot prove that future AI systems will never develop autonomous framework creation capacity. But the current resolution is clear.

4. Cross-model portability not validated. All empirical validation was conducted on specific model families. The degree to which frameworks transfer across architectures remains an open question.

5. Framework composition not studied. When multiple frameworks are injected simultaneously, do they compose additively or interfere? Under what conditions does composition improve performance and under what does it degrade?

These are not weaknesses I hide. They are the research agenda that drives me.

10. Closing: The End of the Digital Pidgin

For four years, since the public emergence of large language models in late 2022, the dominant interaction paradigm has been a form of digital pidgin: an impoverished command language that exploits a fraction of available capabilities, produces generic and frequently unreliable output, and treats the most sophisticated language processing systems in human history as glorified command executors.

Context engineering improved the situation by enriching the model's informational environment. But it left the fundamental problem untouched: providing more information does not teach reasoning.

Digital Craftsmanship provides the epistemic praxis. Framework Injection provides the central mechanism. The Adversarial Tempering Protocol provides the validation discipline.

These contributions are grounded in 2,500 years of philosophical inquiry (Plato, Aristotle, Wittgenstein), supported by seven independent theoretical foundations from developmental psychology to linguistic typology, validated across six professional domains, and organized into a Lakatosian research program — the Artisanal Intelligence Program — designed to evolve through progressive problem shifts while maintaining its core commitments.

The era of digital pidgin is ending. The era of digital craftsmanship is beginning.

Stop commanding AI. Start educating it.
